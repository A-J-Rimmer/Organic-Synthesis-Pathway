ALCOHOLS

Prefix: Hydroxy-

Suffix: -ol



Testing for the Functional Group:

-----Add a small piece of sodium metal.

-----POSITIVE RESULT:

-----Effervescence.

-----NOTE: This result is not unique to alcohols (e.g. Carboxylic acids also effervesce).



Required Information:

PHYSICAL PROPERTIES:

Melting/Boiling Points: Alcohols have higher melting and boiling points than alkanes with the same number of carbon atoms, as the -OH groups cause hydrogen bonding to occur.

Solubility: The -OH group allows short-chain alcohols to dissolve in water- however, the carbon chain is non-polar (and therefore insoluble), and so as the length of the chain increases, the more it inhibits dissolution, and hence solubility decreases.


ETHANOL AS A BIOFUEL:

Glucose can be broken down by enzymes (e.g. zymase) in yeast into ethanol. This glucose comes from carbohydrates in crops such as sugar cane/beet. This reaction is an example of anaerobic respiration:

C6H12O6 → 2C2H5OH + 2CO2

The resulting mixture is ~15% ethanol, (as the enzymes cannot function at higher concentrations) which can be separated via fractional distillation and used as a biofuel.

Ethanol can also be made via the hydration of ethene from crude oil. This method is faster, can occur continuously, and gives a purer product. However, crude oil is a non-renewable source, and when oil reserves are depleted, it may become more economical to make ethanol by the fermentation of sugars.


OXIDATION:

Tertiary alcohols are not easily oxidised, as this requires a C-C bond to break (rather than a C-H bond). However, primary and secondary alcohols can be oxidised via potassium dichromate (K2Cr2O7) and sulphuric acid (H2SO4).

PRIMARY ALCOHOL → ALDEHYDE → CARBOXYLIC ACID*

PRIMARY ALCOHOL → KETONE*

*No further oxidation occurs at this stage, for the same reason as tertiary alcohols.
